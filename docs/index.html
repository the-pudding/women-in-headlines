Headlines about women are more sensational

Sensationalism is an editorial tactic. Although its use is rising, it is not new. The words in a sensational headline are carefully chosen for the purpose of enticing readers. After all, a story titled â€˜Woman, 28, charged after 'stabbing' in Barnsley town centreâ€™ would potentially garner a semi-conscious glance from its reader while one titled â€˜Woman screamed 'KILL KILL KILL!' while repeatedly stabbing a man in Barnsley market attackâ€™ is more likely to see eyes widening and fingers clicking.

But, is this sensationalism more or less prominent in women-focused headlines?

To find out, we used sentiment analysis, a machine learning technique to quantify emotion in text, to rate every headline with a polarity score. A headline with either extremely positive or extremely negative sentiment is considered sensational and hence, is given a higher polarity score. We then compared the polarity of headlines about women with all other headlines for each publication.

We ranked each outlet by the of headlines about women and the between headlines about women and other headlines.

We found that many outlets sensationalise news about women more than other topics. These outlets far exceed the number of outlets that do the opposite. The outlets that sensationalize headlines about women less are not all reporting news first hand, such as Nature or FiveThirtyEight, which do not deal with the everyday news but go deeper into significant topical trends.

We measure polarity by performing sentiment analysis on each headline using the Vader python package, where each headline gets a sentiment score from -1 to 1 (from more negative to more positive). Because we are interested in polarity, we take the absolute value of each headline's score.

News outlet headline bias

While the theme of crime and violence got us delving into how sensational women-centred news is, the theme of gendered language led to the idea of measuring bias.

In March of 1968 Martha Weinman Learâ€™s piece was published in The New York times titled â€œThe Second Feminist Waveâ€. We measure gender bias by tracking the combined occurrence of gendered language and social stereotypes usually associated with women. We do this in two steps:

1) We check if a headline contains gendered language (i.e. 'spokeswoman', 'chairwoman', 'she', 'her', 'bride', 'daughter', 'daughters', 'female', 'fiancee', 'girl', 'girlfriend' etc.).

2) If it contains gendered language, we then count the number of words that are considered to be social stereotypes about women (i.e. 'weak', 'modest', 'virgin', 'slut', 'whore', 'sexy', 'feminine', 'sensitive', 'emotional', 'gentle', 'soft', 'pretty', 'bitch', 'sexual' etc.).

Finally, we normalize this count for all headlines within each outlet as a score between 0 and 1, and we aggregate (i.e. average) this score for each outlet.

Bias-less news?

The story of when women make headlines, like its title, is incomplete. It changes over time and space and allows the reader to keep re-interpreting and completing the title with every new headline, new country and new year they read about. Like most stories about people, it is full of contradictions. It is violent, cruel, sensational, biased, hopeful and empowering although not all of them in equal measure. At the end of this, we believe it is only fitting to leave you with two contradicting thoughts.

First is one of Virginia Woolfâ€™s meditation on language. We acknowledge that not all of her views were inclusive and progressive but we believe her thoughts on language still bear relevance to this story. â€œSince the only test of truth is length of life, and since words survive the chops and changes of time longer than any other substance, therefore they are the truest.â€ The words we consume shape the lens we use to look at society, and in this case, women. Although the words may disappear to become yesterdayâ€™s news, the lens stays on. This dissection of words demonstrates that the words used to talk about women making headlines can be biased, reinforcing existing stereotypes or unnecessarily underlining the gender of the person where there is no relevance. They can be sensational, taunting the reader to be shocked instead of inspiring them to care. They show that for every step forward with women becoming empowered, we take two steps back with women becoming victims of crime and violence. Whatâ€™s more, the lens formed from these words often blindsides the reader to the perpetrators, making this issue a â€˜womenâ€™s securityâ€™ problem rather than a â€˜societal safetyâ€™ problem. If these words are the truest testament of our time, then we have a responsibility to examine, scrutinize, reflect and if possible, correct them before they become the lens we use to look at women.

The second thought is from the East; a Chan Buddhistâ€™s meditation on language, attributed to some and modified by many along the way, says that â€œWhen a person points at the moon, the fool looks at the finger.â€ The moon here is the truth and the finger is language. Language can only point someone to the truth, it is not the truth in itself. After all, we can see the moon without anyone pointing us to it, can we not? After living through a deadly pandemic for over two years, we should know that no words can do justice to the loneliness, despair or grief that took over the world, they can only point someone to the general direction of our mental state. No words, sensational or not, biased or not, can truly explain the emotion, thought and spirit of an event when women make headlines, because words can only approximate.

To visualize this contradiction that words stand the test of time and that they are only a distant echo of the time in which they are relevant, we mapped how words in headlines about women change over time. It turns out, from a time when married women, by law, were considered the property of a man, to single women owning more property as a group than single men, society has come a long way.

Methods

To build the dataset of headlines we scraped data from Google News using RapidAPI, from the top 50 most visited publications according to SimilarWeb in the United States of America, United Kingdom, India and South Africa. We collected all the headlines tagged with the keywords synonymous with women.

Gendered language and bias calculation: To categorise words as gendered, we manually curated two dictionaries - gendered words and words that denote gender stereotypes. This was curated using existing research such as the work done by Huimin Xu and team, published under the title â€˜The Cinderella Complexâ€™ and the incredible research done by The Swaddle team. The methodology used to calculate bias was borrowed from the research done by Yasmeen Hitti and team, published under the title â€˜Proposed Taxonomy for Gender Bias in Textâ€™.

Theme dictionaries: The dictionaries of crime and violence, empowerment and race, ethnicity and identity were curated manually. In cases where the word had more than one contextual usage, we only classified those words that belonged to the same theme in no less than 90% of the cases. To process the words, we used existing NLP packages with modified stop words.

Polarity analysis: For analysing the polarity of each headline we used vaderSentiment. For the polarity comparison, we scraped headlines without any keyword tag from the top 50 publications across these countries. However, the number of headlines we used to calculate the baseline polarity is roughly one third of the headlines we used to calculate the polarity of womanâ€™s news.

There were far more than 1,231 unique words in the original dataset. We filtered most of them manually making the analysis observer dependent and imperfect.
