When Women Make Headlines

A visual essay about the (mis)representation of women in the news

By Leonardo Nicoletti and Sahiti Sarva

As observed in a lot of competitive and high paying fields, there is proven lack of representation of both women and racial minorities in newsrooms. This unbalance is reflected in the news articles that we read every day, where women are often described in ways that reinforce social stereotypes and deepen gender biases. Extensive reporting on this issue in different forms - open editorials, journal papers and academic articles - has pushed people to think about the exacerbating effect that news outlets is having on gender misrepresentation (reference). Still, many questions remain unanswered:

  • What is the extent of gender bias and polarity across influential news outlets?
  • How prevalent are specific topics around the topic of women across different countries?
  • What are the most commonly used words to talk about women in headlines across different countries?
  • What kind of words are used in conjunction with each other around the topic of women?

Since 2005, a total of 1,231 unique words have been used by Indian, South African, English, and American publishers in headlines that report about women. These words are arranged here in a decreasing order of their frequency.

The same word appears at different ranks in different countries, like change, wear, or force.

These countries, although former British colonies, are culturally different. The United States has more stories around the word sue.

It could be because the act is not as common in a country like India or South Africa. Apart from domestic reporting of women involved in suing, the news of American and British residents suing also gained international fame from time to time, like when Two women sued Jeffrey Epstein's estate for $100 million over alleged sex abuse and when Rihanna sued her father for exploiting her name.

Women, especially those as famous as Rihanna, make the news in their multiple identities, as a celebrity, and from time to time, as one of those rare Black women dominating the fashion industry.

Black women, a marginalized group in the USA, UK and South Africa dominate the news in these countries. Most references to black women in India are from the international news coverage. The most recently independent country of South Africa, still recovering from the impact of apartheid, is proudly reporting about their prominent black female doctors, pioneering black female philosophy PhD candidates, while also reporting the events that continue to affect this community.

On the other hand, in a country like India, the racial divide is not as contentious as the religious one.

There are more Indian stories around the word Muslim compared to the UK, USA and South Africa. This is because along with international news being reported in India, the local news is also reported with specific mention to the religion of the storys subject. Like Black women, Muslim women also are affected by intersectionality, that is, they are at a greater disadvantage within society because of gender and racial or religious biases. So any event that hurts the community, like acts of violence, or helps a community, like Rashida Tlaib ad Ilhan Omar becoming the first Mulsim women in Congress, makes the headlines.

Speaking of firsts, the first similarity we noticed between all the countries is the high frequency of the word first. If one were to categorize words in the English language, first would be in the pile of words that are so commonly used that they carry very little information, much like: a, the, is and are. In fact, most algorithms used to analyze data, categorize first as a stop word and remove it from the interesting word pile without a second thought.

Except, with women-centred headlines, for every story told about a first lady, first kiss or first humanoid, there are thousands of stories, nearly 8,000 to be precise, about trailblazing women that shattered glass ceilings. In the recent past, outside Kamala Harris widely covered ascension to Vice-Presidency, Zuzana Caputova became Slovakias first female President, Carrie Lam was named Hong Kongs first female leader and Sahle-Work Zewde was chosen to be Ethiopias first female defence minister.

Flip a page or scroll a little more and you will see women from different races, ethnicities, and identities, becoming the first to lead troops, win medals, fly fighter jets, drive cars, and most of all, raise their voices.

These small steps for women did not always lead to leaps for womankind. The first women to come into power had to work with men who could not recall ever reporting to a woman, the ones who were appointed to lead were up against an 800-year old history of the chair belonging to men and, the ones who raised their voices, calling out for safer spaces, had to take on the task of educating men.

These pieces serve as a reminder of another one of Audre Lordes meditations - The oppressed, objectified groups are expected to stretch out and bridge the gap between the actualities of their lives and the consciousness of the oppressor.

An angry Audre Lorde may have written this way back in the 1980s, but this leads us perfectly to the biggest similarity between the four countries, and the most used word among all headlines, man.

The word man appears more than 30,000 times and looking at the other words in the top 5 words for every country, shows that men make headlines along with women significantly more during horrific events than in historic ones.

We categorized the words used to report women-centered news into different themes. The most common headlines about women revolve around crime and violence.

There are nearly 4,000 stories of death, 6,000 stories of rape and more than 10,000 stories of killing. Although there are stories where women were causing the crime, most of the stories are about women who were at the receiving end of the violence.

These headlines disproportionately focus on the victim compared to the perpetrator. By regularly omitting the cause of the violence and choosing to focus on the victims circumstances, choices and character, the issue becomes about the protection of women rather than the perversion of the perpetrator. When talking about the issue of protection of women, the narrative is often framed, not around protecting every womxn*, but protecting the rights of a countrys daughters, mothers and wives.

This brings us to the second most prevalent theme in the set of words: gendered language. The English language necessitates the use of gendered words and labels to define certain roles in society. In doing so, it unites those who choose to accept the label while othering the rest. Daughter, mother and wife rarely invoke the image of a transgender or non-binary person in a readers mind. While these labels are explicitly gendered, some other words merely become associated with a gender because of underlying stereotypes.

We found words like body, child and marry repeatedly becoming the topics of conversation in women-centred headlines. When women break out of these stereotypes and make headlines, the stories are told of a woman judge, a woman police officer, or a woman actor, lest the reader turn to the default, implicit male worldview encoded in the English language.

The lifeboat that kept us wading through these words to identify themes, are women reclaiming their positions within titles of stories and refusing to remain in the margins. Stories of women, from Black, Dalit or, transgender communities, leading this reclamation in Parliaments, Congresses, High Courts, cities and capitals pushed us to identify three more themes: empowerment, people and places, and finally race, ethnicity and identity.

This process of reclaiming space also led to reclaiming some words that once belonged in a certain theme but do not anymore.

Slay, for instance, started off as a purely violent word. Over time, it started to refer to slay queens, a term used to describe women who were gold diggers. Today, women and the LGBTQIA+ community have reclaimed the word to mean someone who is just very good at what they do. Within these headlines, we see both the stories of women slayed by a man and women slaying a look, a game or outdated standards of beauty.

Many such words fought off attempts to be boxed into themes and chose to remain in the grey area. Feel free to explore them on your own and drop us a note if you think you found another theme.

Finally, we compared the frequency of words across themes. It turns out, for every empowering story, we read nearly two stories of crime and violence.

It is these grounds that inform this study. The idea that we are what we consume is the ground from which we rise.

While news consumption can influence our perceptions, news headlines allow a piece to turn from an article to a story. Headlines are designed to be catchy, sensational and with the advent of 24/7 news cycles, anxiety inducing. They make an imprint on our minds. In this piece, we used data from more than 50,000 headlines from the news outlets of 4 different countries to illustrate how women are represented in news headlines around the world.

Headlines about women are more polarizing

Clearly, newsrooms choose strong language and topics to communicate stories about women subjects. Wait a second... isn't that what newsrooms are all about? To only share high impact stories, so as to capture the reader's attention in the shortest time possible? This gives reason to think that all headlines are polarizing, not just those about women.

With this hypothesis in mind, or team went on to analyze the language used in headlines taken at random (regarding just about any topic) and compared it the language that is used in headlines that portray women subjects. We found, that on average, across more than 200 newsrooms In 4 continents, headlines that speak about women subjects are 40% more polarizing than the average headline.

We measure gender bias by tracking the combined occurrence of gendered language and social stereotypes usually associated with women. We do this in two steps:

1) We check if a headline contains gendered language (i.e. 'spokeswoman', 'chairwoman', 'she', 'her', 'bride', 'daughter', 'daughters', 'female', 'fiancee', 'girl', 'girlfriend' etc.).

2) If it contains gendered language, we then count the number of words that are considered to be social stereotypes about women (i.e. 'weak', 'modest', 'virgin', 'slut', 'whore', 'sexy', 'feminine', 'sensitive', 'emotional', 'gentle', 'soft', 'pretty', 'bitch', 'sexual' etc.).

Finally, we normalize this count for all headlines within each outlet as a score between 0 and 1, and we aggregate (i.e. average) this score for each outlet.

We measure polarity by performing sentiment analysis on each headline using the Vader python package, where each headline gets a sentiment score from -1 to 1 (from more negative to more positive). Because we are interested in polarity, we take the absolute value of each headline's score.

Bias-less news?

News articles have the potential to define perceptions and shape the general public opinion around different topics. At a time when women need allies more than ever before, news outlets can be a powerful partner in fostering an unbiased public opinion about women. In practice, however, they are not.

For a given news outlet, the chart below shows the amount of gender bias and polarity used in headlines of that outlet. Bubbles represent outlets, and are sized by the size of their audience.

For each headline we measure polarity by using sentiment analysis, a machine learning technique that is used to detect emotional affect in text. In this analysis we chose to consider the emotional strength of each headline as a proxy for polarity (irrespective of whether the emotion is positive or negative). By aggregating the polarity scores of an outlet's headlines we can rank each outlet on the basis of their average polarity.

For each headline we measure bias by tracking the combined occurrence of gendered language and social stereotypes usually associated with women. Gendered language includes gendered pronouns (i.e. she, her, mrs, mis etc.) and gendered nouns (i.e. girl, spokeswoman, girlfriend etc.). Examples of words that we categorize as social stereotypes are slut , emotional, sensitive, or married, among many others. By counting these co-occurrences we are able to assign a gender bias score to each headline. For example, a recent headline by the Daily Mail reads:

'Woman reveals she's thinking about dumping her boyfriend because of his close female friend'

This headline would get a bias score of 4. Finally, by aggregating all headline scores by news outlet we rank each outlet from most biased to least biased.

Methods

The core data source for this visual exploration is a collection of 400,000 headlines retrieved from the Google News API. The analysis presented here covers the January 1st, 2010 to June 11th, 2021 timeframe. The data was processed and analyzed using the Python programming language. This visual essay was designed using the d3.js javascript package. The code used for the entire project, from data-collection and analysis to visualization and web-development can be found in our GitHub Repository.
